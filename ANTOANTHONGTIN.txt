Overview of Information Security
This section establishes information as a key asset and addresses the fundamental need for data protection amidst the complexities of the Internet and the AI era. Tracing its lineage from 4,000 years of classical cryptography, Information Security has evolved into a rigorous academic discipline, anchored by the CIA Triad (Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability) and guided by international standards such as ISO/IEC 27001.

The core curriculum emphasizes the valuation of information, the classification of threats (accidental vs. intentional and passive vs. active), and the identification of systemic vulnerabilities. Furthermore, it explores the basic concepts of defense-in-depth (multi-layered protection) strategies to ensure organizational resilience.

Upon completion, students will understand professional terminology, develop analytical thinking to identify common threats, and acquire foundational skills in configuring and managing basic secure environments. Ultimately, learners will recognize the complementary roles of human factors and technological controls in safeguarding modern digital assets

Decoding the Mathematics of Security Systems
This section establishes the rigorous mathematical foundations required to understand, analyze, and design secure cryptographic mechanisms. The curriculum begins with fundamental logical operators (AND, OR, NOT, XOR)  and branches into core concepts of number theory and abstract algebra, including prime numbers, modular arithmetic, extended Euclidean algorithm, Fermat's little theorem, and linear modular equations. Furthermore, it explores algebraic structures such as groups, rings, and finite fields, alongside computationally hard problems like integer factorization and discrete logarithms, culminating in the study of elliptic curves. These mathematical frameworks serve as the absolute foundation powering symmetric cryptography, asymmetric cryptography, cryptographic hash functions, and digital signatures.

Upon completion, students will understand foundational mathematical frameworks underpinning security systems, develop analytical thinking to recognize cryptographic strength through computational complexity, and acquire foundational skills in applying basic algebraic and number-theoretic properties to security engineering. Ultimately, learners will recognize the complementary roles of discrete logical operations and abstract mathematical structures in constructing secure cryptographic controls that protect modern digital assets.

Symmetric Cryptography: Shared-key Principles and Digital Security Performance
This section delivers a rigorous examination of private-key cryptographic frameworks, focusing on the core mechanisms designed to achieve high-performance confidentiality in data storage and transmission. The curriculum contrasts the structural paradigms of block ciphers and stream ciphers, exploring classical design principles such as the Feistel network cipher model and the Substitution-Permutation Network (SPN) foundational model for AES. Special emphasis is placed on the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) as the premier, modern concrete algorithm utilized globally to protect contemporary digital assets and data. Furthermore, the section evaluates the intrinsic advantages and disadvantages of symmetric cryptography, particularly regarding its computational efficiency and the complexities of key distribution. To address the secure processing of large-scale data, students will analyze various block cipher modes of operation, including Electronic Codebook (ECB), Cipher Block Chaining (CBC), and Counter Mode (CTR), along with other advanced operational variants.

Upon completion, students will understand the structural design principles of modern symmetric cryptosystems, develop the ability to select appropriate cryptographic modes based on data scale and security requirements, and acquire foundational skills in configuring basic AES settings to safeguard sensitive enterprise environments. Ultimately, learners will recognize the complementary roles of structural confusion-diffusion mechanisms and optimized operational modes in achieving data confidentiality without compromising modern computational efficiency.

Asymmetric Cryptography: Public-key Principles and Global Authentication Foundations
This section provides a rigorous, in-depth analysis of public-key cryptosystems and their critical role in scaling global security architectures and trust infrastructures. The curriculum dissects the underlying algorithmic mechanisms, operational principles, mathematical foundations, and explicit intended use cases of prominent asymmetric paradigms, focusing heavily on Rivest-Shamir-Adleman (RSA) and Elliptic Curve Cryptography (ECC). Students will evaluate the distinctive architectural trade-offs, advantages, and disadvantages intrinsic to each framework—such as RSA's computational reliance on large integer factorization versus ECC's superior efficiency driven by the elliptic curve discrete logarithm problem. Through this comparative lens, the section explores how these asymmetric principles resolve the complex key-distribution challenges of symmetric networks, thereby establishing the mathematical bedrock for secure key exchange, robust data confidentiality, and global entity authentication.

Upon completion, students will understand the mathematical mechanics and core operational workflows of diverse asymmetric algorithms, develop the ability to explain the security-versus-performance trade-offs between RSA and ECC across varied infrastructural constraints, and acquire foundational skills in deploying basic public-key configurations for scalable enterprise deployment. Ultimately, learners will recognize the complementary roles of computationally hard mathematical problems and asymmetric key pairs in architecting secure, non-repudiable global communication channels.

Hash Functions: The Mechanism for Information Immutability
This section delivers a comprehensive investigation into cryptographic hash functions and their role as the primary mechanism for ensuring data integrity and informational immutability. The curriculum explores the core mathematical properties of cryptographic hashes, including pre-image resistance, second pre-image resistance, and collision resistance. Students will analyze structural methodologies and construction models, focusing heavily on the Merkle-Damgård iterative framework that underpins historical and modern standards. The section provides a critical comparative evaluation of the applications, structural advantages, and security vulnerabilities of legacy systems like SHA-1 against modern alternatives like SHA-2. To demystify the inner mechanics of hashing, the curriculum provides an explicit, step-by-step algorithmic breakdown of SHA-256, guiding students through its block padding, message scheduling, logical bitwise operations, and compression functions.

Upon completion, students will understand the core mathematical properties and architectural designs governing hash algorithms, develop the ability to explain the security margins of SHA-1 and SHA-2 under collision attack models, and acquire foundational skills in tracing and demonstrating the computational workflows of SHA-256. Ultimately, learners will recognize the complementary roles of collision-resistant data compression and iterative algebraic chaining in preserving structural data integrity across untrusted networks.

Digital Signatures: The Convergence of Hashing and Asymmetric Cryptography
This section delivers a definitive exploration of digital signature schemes, analyzing them as the architectural convergence point where cryptographic hash functions and asymmetric algorithms combine to enforce data integrity, source authentication, and non-repudiation. The curriculum underscores the vital importance of digital signatures in securing electronic commerce, software distribution, legal frameworks, and global trust infrastructures. Students will evaluate pioneering and modern concrete signing algorithms through detailed algebraic and procedural breakdowns. The section traces the historical evolution of signature designs, beginning with the foundational ElGamal signature scheme—examining its technical vulnerabilities, susceptibility to existential forgery, and subsequent obsolescence. This analysis transitions to the highly efficient Schnorr signature framework and the standardized Digital Signature Algorithm (DSA). Furthermore, special focus is placed on the Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm (ECDSA), mapping out its mathematical mechanics, computational efficiency, and dominant position in securing modern enterprise networks and distributed ledger environments.
Upon completion, students will understand the structural workflows and algebraic verification models of major signature frameworks, explain historical cryptographic flaws such as the existential forgery of the original ElGamal scheme, and acquire foundational skills in configuring and verifying basic ECDSA configurations. Ultimately, learners will recognize the symbiotic roles of collision-resistant data compression and public-key pair mathematics in establishing secure and trustworthy digital signatures across modern digital architectures

Final Examination
The final examination will be administered in a multiple-choice format. During the examination, students are strictly prohibited from using any Artificial Intelligence (AI) tools or assistance.

Following the examination, the instructor reserves the right to randomly select students for an oral defense consisting of three (3) questions. Adjustments to the examination score based on oral performance are defined as follows:

All questions answered correctly: The student passes the oral defense component, and the original multiple-choice score remains unchanged.
One incorrect answer: A deduction of 10% will be applied to the total examination score.
Two incorrect answers: A deduction of 30% will be applied to the total examination score.
Three incorrect answers: A deduction of 50% will be applied to the total examination score.
Special Academic Integrity Provision
If a student achieves a score between 9.0 and 10.0 on the multiple-choice examination but fails to answer any oral defense questions correctly, a strict penalty deduction ranging from 60% to 70% may be applied to the total examination score at the instructor’s discretion.
